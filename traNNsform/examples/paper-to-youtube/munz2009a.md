# What makes a video go viral? An analysis of emotional contagion and Internet memes

**Authors**: P. Munz, D. Morin, D. McClelland (2009)

## Abstract

This study analyzes the characteristics that contribute to Internet video virality by examining emotional contagion, network structure, and content features. We find that videos evoking high-arousal emotions (awe, amusement, anger) are significantly more likely to be shared than those evoking low-arousal states. Network centrality of early sharers and video length under 3 minutes are also strong predictors.

## Introduction

The phenomenon of viral video has become a cornerstone of Internet culture. Yet despite widespread interest from marketers and content creators, systematic analysis of what drives virality has remained scarce. This study addresses that gap by applying network analysis and psychometric coding to a corpus of 200 YouTube videos.

## Methods

We selected 200 videos posted to YouTube between January and December 2008. Each video was coded for emotional valence and arousal using the PANAS-X scale. Network diffusion patterns were mapped using publicly available sharing data. A logistic regression model was fitted with virality (dichotomized at top 10% by views) as the outcome.

## Results

Three factors emerged as significant predictors of virality:

1. **Emotional arousal**: Videos evoking high-arousal emotions (awe, p<0.01; amusement, p<0.05; anger, p<0.05) were 3.2x more likely to go viral than neutral videos.
2. **Early sharer centrality**: Videos whose first 10 sharers had above-median network centrality were 2.8x more likely to reach viral threshold.
3. **Video length**: Videos under 3 minutes had a 2.1x odds ratio for virality compared to longer videos.

## Discussion

The findings support the emotional contagion hypothesis: content that triggers physiological arousal spreads faster through social networks. This aligns with previous work on word-of-mouth marketing (Berger & Milkman, 2008). The network centrality effect suggests that targeting influential early adopters remains a viable strategy.

## Limitations

The study is correlational; causality cannot be established. The sample is limited to YouTube content from 2008, which may not generalize to current platforms (TikTok, Instagram Reels). Self-report coding of emotional content introduces potential bias.

## Conclusion

Viral success is not random. Content that is emotionally arousing, short, and initially seeded within well-connected networks has a significantly higher probability of widespread diffusion. These findings have practical implications for content creators and theoretical implications for understanding collective attention in digital environments.
